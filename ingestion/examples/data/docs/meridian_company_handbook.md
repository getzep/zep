# Company Handbook

This handbook is the canonical introduction to Meridian Robotics for new
employees, partners, and customer teams. It describes who we are, what we
build, who leads the work, and how our supplier and customer relationships
fit together. Keep it close during your first month; almost every question
about the company starts here.

## Who we are

Meridian Robotics designs and manufactures warehouse robots for mid-size
fulfillment companies. The company is headquartered in Portland, Oregon,
where engineering, manufacturing, and operations share one building — a
deliberate choice, because the fastest way to fix a hardware problem is to
walk the prototype to the person who designed it.

We are not trying to automate the largest warehouses in the world. Our
customers run buildings that do twenty to eighty thousand picks a day, and
they need robotics that commission in weeks, not quarters. Everything about
how we build — the products, the supply chain, the support model — follows
from that focus.

## Products

Meridian Robotics sells two products, and the distinction matters in every
customer conversation.

PickPoint One is our hardware product: a robotic picking arm engineered for
small-parts fulfillment zones. It mounts on standard racking, runs on
building power, and is designed so a customer's own maintenance crew can
service it after a one-week commissioning. PickPoint One is sold per unit,
with volume pricing for full-building rollouts.

FleetView is our software product: a monitoring and analytics subscription
for robot fleets. FleetView shows pick rates, fault trends, and per-arm
utilization in real time, and it is the tool our own support engineers use
when a customer calls. FleetView is sold as an annual subscription, priced
by fleet size, and it works with every PickPoint generation we have ever
shipped.

## Active programs

Atlas is the flagship program this year: the next-generation picking arm
that will eventually succeed PickPoint One. Atlas carries a P0 priority,
and Bob Rivera leads it. Early in the program Atlas was known internally as
MR-42; that code name is retired, and documents that still say MR-42 mean
Atlas. The gripper subsystem is the technically hardest part of the
program, and its servo actuators come from our supplier Voltaic Components.

Beacon is the second active program: a migration of our fleet telemetry
pipeline to the architecture FleetView needs for real-time alerting. Beacon
is a P1; when Atlas needs staffing, Beacon is where the people come from.

## Suppliers

Voltaic Components is our most important supplier. Voltaic manufactures the
servo actuators used in the PickPoint One line and in the Atlas prototypes,
and supplies them from its plant in Fremont, California. Standard lead time
from Fremont is eight weeks, which drives most of our production planning.
Carol Diaz is Voltaic's account executive for Meridian Robotics and the
single point of contact for specs, volumes, and dates.

## Customers

Northwind Logistics is a regional fulfillment provider and our lead pilot
customer for PickPoint One. Northwind operates a fulfillment center in
Reno, Nevada, that handles roughly sixty thousand picks a day. Dana
Whitfield, Northwind's operations manager, runs the pilot on their side.

Cascadia Freight is a long-standing FleetView customer. Cascadia runs
FleetView across its distribution sites and works with our support team
through the standard subscription channel. Marcus Webb coordinates the
FleetView rollout on the Cascadia side.

## How we work

Programs get one directly responsible individual, and that name is public
inside the company. Status flows weekly from program leads to Alice Chen
and Priya Sharma. Supplier commitments go through the named account
executive, never through side channels — if a date matters, Carol Diaz
hears about it from us in writing.

When you are unsure who owns something, ask in the #general channel rather
than guessing. The company is small enough that the answer usually arrives
in minutes, and the question itself often improves this handbook.
